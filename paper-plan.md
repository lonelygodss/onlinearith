This work studies a **CIM-oriented accelerator for the FFN layer of large language models**. The main goal is to reduce computation dynamically without relying on conventional structured sparsification, which often hurts LLM accuracy and interacts poorly with low-precision MX-style quantization. Our approach therefore treats **MSD-first digit-pipelined online arithmetic with BSD inner representation** as an execution mechanism inside a CIM macro, rather than as the sole identity of the work. In other words, this is presented as a **CIM paper that uses online arithmetic to make dynamic compute reduction practical**, not as an online-arithmetic paper detached from CIM concerns. (summary.md)

The key motivation is that many existing low-cost inference flows effectively perform **quantization first and sparsification second**, even though the two operations are not cleanly orthogonal in LLM FFNs. Our central idea is to avoid that loose sequential treatment. Instead, we use the cheap scale metadata already available in MX quantization to infer significance early and convert it into a **bounded stop schedule** during computation. In this view, dynamic fine-grained sparsity is not realized as irregular skipping of weights or activations, but as **metadata-driven compute truncation** that remains compatible with regular CIM organization.

The current version of the work intentionally separates **two parallel contribution lines**. The first line is **checkpoint-based truncation**. After experiments, we find that truncation is best placed at **projection checkpoints**, because this gives the best balance among model quality, hardware latency, and control cost. The second line is **FFN-deep pipelining**. Even when truncation is resolved at checkpoints, the FFN is still executed as one producer-consumer streamline so that timing continuity is preserved across `gate_proj`, `up_proj`, `SiLU`, gating, and `down_proj`, and representation transformation is paid mainly at the FFN boundaries rather than repeatedly inside the block. These two lines address different problems: checkpoint truncation determines **how much computation is performed**, while deep pipelining determines **how efficiently that computation moves through the FFN**.

The simulator already supports this interpretation on top of a modified Qwen3-0.6B stack. It implements **MXFP block quantization** (block size 32), uniform and calibrated cycle budgeting, optional dynamic budget deltas derived from combined activation and weight scales, inter-block and intra-block delay modeling, and hardware-facing performance tracing. The same MSD truncation primitive is reused by both calibration and inference, which keeps the optimization target consistent with the distortion model seen at runtime. In deep-pipeline mode, the simulator carries a float-valued numeric carrier together with timing metadata such as the **first valid MSD arrival**, which enables FFN-wide transaction-level timing without the cost of a full persistent BSD-stream simulation across the entire model. This is important because it keeps full-model perplexity evaluation and hardware trace extraction practical at LLM scale. (summary.md) (deep-pipeline.txt)

Under this formulation, the FFN is modeled as a **producer-consumer dataflow**. `gate_proj` and `up_proj` act as producer stages, the nonlinear and gating operations are explicit in-pipeline stages, and `down_proj` is treated as a consumer stage that receives both the intermediate value and its carried timing metadata. This lets the simulator model alignment delay, stage latency, and arrival skew explicitly, while still staying scalable enough for real LLM inference. The nonlinear part is represented by an **8-segment PWL SiLU block**, and timing is propagated across the whole FFN rather than reset independently at every local operator. (summary.md) (deep-pipeline.txt)

Algorithmically, the budget system remains hierarchical. A **uniform budget** is the simplest baseline. **SNR-calibrated budgets** solve for the minimum per-channel cycle allocation that satisfies a target distortion bound. **Fixed-sum calibration** starts from the SNR-min solution and redistributes cycles across channels to reduce total layer error while preserving exactly the same total hardware budget.

With this setup, the main comparison group is now well defined: **dense MX baseline, Wanda 2:4 baseline, uniform-budget MSD, SNR-calibrated budget, fixed-sum calibrated budget, and dynamic budget**. Optional activation-only and weight-only truncation baselines can be added to isolate the benefit of using **combined activation+weight metadata** rather than only one side. This comparison set keeps the story focused. The claim is not that every sparsity method is bad, but that **metadata-driven checkpoint truncation inside an FFN pipeline is a more CIM-compatible way to reduce work than traditional structured sparsification at similar quality targets**.

At the hardware level, the concrete demonstration target is a **32-channel FFN streamline macro**. This macro is described in CIM language: standard online-arithmetic cells form the compute substrate, but the architectural contribution lies in how a CIM-style macro organizes them around a **bounded, metadata-driven stop schedule**. The macro contains small alignment FIFOs, an 8-segment PWL SiLU block, producer stages for the FFN input projections, and a **replay-buffered consumer tile** for `down_proj`. Early termination is therefore implemented as a bounded stop decision derived from metadata and base budgets, rather than from expensive runtime inspection of partial sums. This is the hardware interpretation of dynamic fine-grained sparsity: it becomes a **regular scheduling problem** instead of an irregular skip-control problem. (summary.md) (deep-pipeline.txt)

The scale-up story is also fixed by this description. One 32-channel streamline macro is the **detailed design unit**. A full FFN layer is supported by replicating this macro across the large intermediate dimension in a tensor-parallel CIM style. This lets the work stay concrete at the macro level while still addressing the scale of real LLM FFNs. It also matches the simulation methodology: model accuracy is evaluated at full-model scale, while energy and latency can later be estimated by combining **trace statistics**—such as utilization, zero-block fraction, and cycle histograms—with circuit-level macro costs, instead of requiring a complete full-chip infrastructure from the beginning. (summary.md)

The current simulation results are still preliminary, but they already point to the right direction. SNR-based calibration can keep perplexity nearly lossless at about **30% utilization**, and fixed-sum calibration improves quality further. At the same time, near-lossless operating points show only about **1% zero blocks**, which suggests that the main benefit is not simply shutting down whole blocks. The larger effect comes from reducing **active digit work and active cycles** inside partially useful computation. This is an important signal for future refinement, because it means the hardware story should emphasize **cheap cycle control and bounded stop scheduling**, not only coarse block gating.

Taken together, the current work should be understood as a **CIM-oriented FFN accelerator study** in which **MX quantization and dynamic compute reduction are fused through checkpoint-based MSD truncation**, while an **FFN-deep producer-consumer pipeline** preserves timing continuity and reduces conversion overhead. The algorithmic side decides where limited cycle budget should be spent, the hardware side shows how a regular 32-channel macro can realize that policy, and the simulation side connects perplexity to hardware-facing traces. This version is intentionally stable: later improvements can refine the circuit details, energy model, and calibration policy without changing the core claim of the project.

A compact sentence you can keep reusing is:

> We present a CIM-oriented FFN macro that uses checkpoint-based MSD-first truncation and an FFN-deep producer-consumer pipeline to turn dynamic fine-grained sparsity into a bounded, metadata-driven stop schedule.
